Outlook Goes Agentic: Copilot Now Runs Your Inbox and Calendar

Microsoft has flipped the switch on the most significant Outlook update in years. Starting April 27, Copilot in Outlook is no longer just a drafting assistant — it's a full agentic system that proactively manages your email and calendar without being asked. The rollout is available via the Frontier early-access program across all Outlook endpoints.

On the inbox side, Copilot now triages and prioritizes messages, identifies emails awaiting replies for over 24 hours, drafts polite follow-ups, and can even create inbox rules on the fly. On the calendar side, it goes further: automatically responding to meeting invites based on your preferences, resolving 1:1 scheduling conflicts by rebooking, reserving meeting rooms, and blocking focus time when your schedule allows.

The most striking feature is proactive calendar delegation — Copilot can reschedule your entire week of 1:1s, decline meetings outside your working hours (unless from leadership), and draft agendas tailored to tone and audience. Calendar delegation is currently limited to Outlook for Windows and web.

→ Impact: This transforms Outlook from a tool you use into an agent that works for you. For our product, this raises the bar significantly — competing email clients now need to match not just AI drafting but autonomous inbox and calendar management. The "always-on agent" paradigm that was previously the domain of startups is now built into the world's most deployed enterprise email client.

Microsoft Delays SMTP Basic Auth Shutdown to December 2026

The deadline that email client developers have been watching — Microsoft's retirement of Basic Authentication for SMTP AUTH Client Submission in Exchange Online — has been pushed back again. Originally set for April 30, 2026 (today!), Microsoft quietly updated the timeline: SMTP AUTH Basic Auth behavior remains unchanged through December 2026, when it will be disabled by default for existing tenants.

The delay reflects the reality that many legacy systems, multifunction printers, IoT devices, and older email clients still depend on Basic Auth. App passwords, which many users rely on as a workaround, will also stop working once the retirement takes effect. Older Outlook versions (2007, 2010, 2013 MSI) cannot be updated to support OAuth 2.0.

Google completed its own Basic Auth retirement for Gmail on March 14, 2025, over a year ahead of Microsoft's moving target.

→ Impact: The delay gives third-party email clients more breathing room to ensure OAuth 2.0 compliance, but the direction is clear — Basic Auth is dying. For the team, this is a non-event if OAuth support is already solid, but worth monitoring for any customer support volume around authentication issues as the December deadline approaches.
