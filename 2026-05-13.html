Googlebook's "Magic Pointer" — DeepMind Turns the Cursor Into an AI Agent for Email & Calendar

Google didn't just announce a new laptop category at The Android Show — it reimagined the most fundamental input device on a computer. Built with Google DeepMind, the Googlebook's "Magic Pointer" replaces the traditional cursor with an AI-powered pointing device that understands context, anticipates intent, and surfaces Gemini intelligence with a simple wiggle.

The implications for email and calendar workflows are immediate: hover over a date mentioned in an email and Gemini suggests creating a calendar event. Select a restaurant name and get a reservation link. Point at a flight confirmation and see it on a map with drive-time estimates. DeepMind describes the vision as a pointer that understands not just what it's pointing at, but why it matters to you — drawing on Gmail, Calendar, and your broader Google graph.

The first Googlebooks from Acer, Asus, Dell, HP, and Lenovo ship this fall, running Android with full app support plus phone-app streaming via "Cast my apps." Custom widgets can be generated by prompting Gemini — pulling live data from Gmail and Calendar into dashboard tiles on the home screen.

→ Impact: This is the cursor itself becoming an email-and-calendar AI agent. When the most basic interaction layer on a laptop is context-aware and connected to your inbox, the value of a dedicated email client shifts from "organizing messages" to "delivering intelligence the OS can't." The team needs to think about what our product offers that a smart cursor never could — depth of workflow, cross-account intelligence, and privacy.

Mira Murati's Thinking Machines Lab Unveils "Interaction Models" — Full-Duplex AI That Responds in 0.4 Seconds

The former CTO of OpenAI just introduced a fundamentally new class of AI model. Thinking Machines Lab's "interaction models" are trained from scratch to handle real-time, full-duplex communication — the model listens, speaks, sees, and pauses naturally, all simultaneously, rather than bolting real-time interaction onto a turn-based architecture.

The numbers are striking: TML-Interaction-Small, a 276-billion-parameter Mixture-of-Experts model (12B active), achieves a 0.40-second response latency, versus OpenAI's GPT-Realtime-2.0 at 1.18 seconds. The model processes audio, video, and text in parallel 200-millisecond blocks, treating input and output as continuous, overlapping streams rather than sequential turns.

A research preview is expected within months, with a broader release later this year. Murati's lab published the architecture and benchmarks before scaling the model — a deliberate signal that this isn't vaporware.

→ Impact: Full-duplex interaction models are the missing piece for AI meeting assistants that actually participate rather than passively transcribe. Imagine a meeting copilot that listens, takes notes, and responds to questions in real time — all in under half a second. This technology will reshape the meeting notes category within 12–18 months, and our product should be watching for integration opportunities.

AI Meeting Recorders Face Federal Court — Otter.ai and Fireflies Consent Lawsuits Head to Hearing

The legal reckoning for AI meeting recorders arrives next week. A motion-to-dismiss hearing is scheduled for May 20 at the San Jose federal courthouse, addressing consent lawsuits filed against Otter.ai and Fireflies.ai over recording practices. The core legal question: does joining a meeting where an AI bot is present constitute consent to being recorded and transcribed?

The lawsuits allege violations of state wiretapping and eavesdropping statutes, arguing that participants were not adequately informed that AI tools were capturing their conversations. If the court allows the cases to proceed, it could establish precedent that reshapes how every AI meeting assistant handles consent, notification, and data retention.

The market is already feeling the chill: Reddit threads from this month show users treating meeting note tools as specialized rather than interchangeable, with privacy-conscious users gravitating toward tools like Granola that capture notes locally without sending bots into calls.

→ Impact: A ruling that tightens consent requirements for AI meeting recorders would benefit products with privacy-first architectures. If our meeting notes feature processes data on-device or with explicit user control, that becomes a selling point as competitors scramble to comply. Worth watching the May 20 hearing closely.
